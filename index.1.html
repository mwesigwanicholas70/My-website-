📏 1. Measurement & Kinematics

Physical Quantities & Units

Definition: A physical quantity is a property of an object or phenomenon that can be measured. It has both magnitude and unit.

SI Base Units:

Quantity Symbol SI Unit Abbreviation
Length l Metre m
Mass m Kilogram kg
Time t Second s
Current I Ampere A
Temperature T Kelvin K

Motion (Kinematics)

Displacement: Distance moved in a specific direction (vector quantity). Velocity: Rate of change of displacement (vector). v = s/t Acceleration: Rate of change of velocity (vector). a = Δv/t = (v-u)/t
Equations of Motion (constant acceleration): v = u + at s = ut + ½at² v² = u² + 2as
Key Points:
• u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time, s = displacement • Positive direction is chosen as reference • Deceleration is negative acceleration

💪 2. Forces & Motion (Newton's Laws)

Newton's Laws of Motion

First Law (Law of Inertia):

"An object at rest remains at rest and an object in motion remains in motion unless acted upon by an external force."

Implication: An object has inertia - resistance to change in motion.

Second Law:

"The rate of change of momentum is directly proportional to the applied force."

F = ma (Force = Mass × Acceleration)

Third Law:

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Example: When you push a wall, the wall pushes back with equal force.

Types of Forces

  • Gravitational Force: Force of attraction between masses. F = mg (weight)
  • Friction: Resistance to motion. F = μN (μ = coefficient of friction, N = normal force)
  • Tension: Force in a rope or string pulling an object
  • Normal Force: Perpendicular force from a surface
  • Applied Force: Force directly applied to an object
  • Air Resistance: Friction with air particles

⚡ 3. Energy, Work & Power

Work & Energy

Work: Product of force and displacement in the direction of force.
W = F × s × cos(θ)
Where θ is the angle between force and displacement.
Energy: Capacity to do work. Measured in Joules (J).

Types of Energy:

  • Kinetic Energy: Energy due to motion. KE = ½mv²
  • Potential Energy: Energy due to position. PE = mgh
  • Elastic PE: Energy in stretched spring. PE = ½kx²
  • Heat Energy: Energy from temperature
  • Chemical Energy: Energy in chemical bonds
  • Nuclear Energy: Energy in atomic nucleus
Law of Conservation of Energy:
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. Total Energy = Constant

Power

Power: Rate of doing work or rate of energy transfer.
P = W/t (Watts, W)
Also: P = Fv (Power = Force × Velocity)

📊 4. Pressure

Pressure Definition & Formulas

Pressure: Force per unit area perpendicular to the surface.
P = F/A (Pascal, Pa)
1 Pa = 1 N/m²

Types of Pressure:

  • Atmospheric Pressure: Pressure of air above. 101,325 Pa = 1 atm = 760 mmHg
  • Gauge Pressure: Pressure above atmospheric
  • Absolute Pressure: Total pressure (gauge + atmospheric)
Pressure in Liquids: P = ρgh Where ρ = density, g = acceleration due to gravity, h = height

🎯 5. Density & Relative Density

Density

Density: Mass per unit volume.
ρ = m/V (kg/m³ or g/cm³)

Densities of Common Substances (at 20°C):

Substance Density (kg/m³)
Water 1000
Aluminum 2700
Iron 7800
Copper 8900
Lead 11,300
Mercury 13,600
Relative Density (Specific Gravity): Ratio of density of substance to density of water.
Relative Density = Density of substance / Density of water
No units (dimensionless)

🌊 6. Waves & Oscillations

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

SHM: Motion where acceleration is directly proportional to displacement and directed toward the equilibrium position.
a = -ω²x
Where ω = angular frequency = 2πf

Characteristics of SHM:

  • Oscillation about a fixed point (equilibrium)
  • Maximum displacement = Amplitude (A)
  • Time for one complete oscillation = Period (T)
  • Number of oscillations per second = Frequency (f)
  • T = 1/f

Wave Properties

Wave: Disturbance that travels through space, carrying energy.

Types of Waves:

  • Transverse Waves: Vibration perpendicular to wave direction (light, water waves)
  • Longitudinal Waves: Vibration parallel to wave direction (sound, ultrasound)
Wave Equation: v = fλ Where v = wave speed, f = frequency, λ = wavelength

Wave Phenomena:

  • Reflection: Wave bounces back from surface
  • Refraction: Wave bends when moving between different media
  • Diffraction: Wave bends around obstacles or through gaps
  • Interference: Waves combine to produce constructive or destructive interference
  • Polarization: Wave vibration in one plane (transverse waves only)

🔊 7. Sound

Sound Waves

Sound: Longitudinal wave created by vibrating objects, travels through elastic media.

Speed of Sound in Different Media (at 20°C):

  • Air: ~340 m/s
  • Water: ~1,480 m/s
  • Steel: ~5,000 m/s
Frequency Range: • Infrasound: < 20 Hz • Audible: 20 Hz - 20,000 Hz • Ultrasound: > 20,000 Hz

Sound Phenomena:

  • Echo: Sound reflection from distant surface (returns after noticeable delay)
  • Reverberation: Multiple reflections creating lingering sound
  • Doppler Effect: Frequency change when source/observer moves

💡 8. Light & Optics

Nature of Light

Light: Electromagnetic wave that is visible to human eye. Speed in vacuum = 3×10⁸ m/s

Electromagnetic Spectrum (in order of increasing frequency):

  • Radio waves (longest wavelength)
  • Microwaves
  • Infrared
  • Visible light (ROYGBIV)
  • Ultraviolet
  • X-rays
  • Gamma rays (shortest wavelength)

Reflection of Light

Laws of Reflection:

  1. Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection
  2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in same plane
  3. Angles measured from normal (perpendicular)

Types of Mirrors:

  • Plane Mirror: Flat surface. Image: virtual, erect, same size, laterally inverted
  • Concave Mirror: Converging. Can form real or virtual images
  • Convex Mirror: Diverging. Always forms virtual, erect, diminished image
Mirror Formula: 1/f = 1/u + 1/v Where f = focal length, u = object distance, v = image distance

Refraction of Light

Refraction: Bending of light when it passes from one medium to another of different optical density.
Snell's Law: n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂ Where n = refractive index, θ = angle from normal

Refractive Index of Common Substances:

  • Air: 1.0
  • Water: 1.33
  • Glass: 1.5
  • Diamond: 2.42
Critical Angle: Angle of incidence at which refracted angle = 90°. Beyond this, total internal reflection occurs.
sinθc = n₂/n₁ (when n₂ < n₁)

Lenses

Types of Lenses:

  • Convex (Converging): Thicker at center. Converges parallel rays to focal point
  • Concave (Diverging): Thinner at center. Diverges parallel rays
Lens Formula: 1/f = 1/u + 1/v Power of lens P = 1/f (in Diopters, D)

Lens Magnification:

m = v/u = -height of image / height of object
• m > 1: Image magnified • m < 1: Image reduced • m negative: Image inverted (real)

⚡ 9. Electricity

Electric Charge & Current

Electric Charge: Property of matter (electrons have negative, protons positive). Measured in Coulombs (C).
Q = ne (charge = number of electrons × electron charge) e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Electric Current: Flow of charge per unit time.
I = Q/t (Amperes, A)

Potential Difference & Resistance

Potential Difference (Voltage): Energy given to charge per unit charge.
V = W/Q (Volts, V)
Resistance: Opposition to flow of current.
R = V/I (Ohms, Ω)

Factors Affecting Resistance:

R = ρl/A Where ρ = resistivity, l = length, A = cross-sectional area

EMF & Internal Resistance

EMF (Electromotive Force): Energy provided by source per unit charge.
E = I(R + r) Where R = external resistance, r = internal resistance

Terminal Voltage:

V = E - Ir

Electrical Power & Energy

Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R (Watts, W) Energy: E = Pt = VIt (Joules, J)

Circuits

Series Circuits:

  • Same current through all components
  • Total resistance: R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃...
  • Voltage divides: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃...

Parallel Circuits:

  • Same voltage across all components
  • Total resistance: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃...
  • Current divides: I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃...

🧲 10. Magnetism

Magnetic Fields & Forces

Magnetic Field: Region where magnetic forces are exerted on magnetic poles or moving charges.

Sources of Magnetic Fields:

  • Permanent magnets (always magnetized)
  • Current-carrying conductors (create field around wire)
  • Electromagnets (coil with current)
  • Earth (geomagnetic field)
Magnetic Force on Current-Carrying Conductor:
F = BIl sinθ Where B = magnetic field strength, I = current, l = length of conductor, θ = angle

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (Motor Rule):

  • Thumb: Direction of force/motion
  • First finger: Magnetic field direction (N to S)
  • Second finger: Current direction

Electromagnetic Induction

Electromagnetic Induction: Generation of EMF when magnetic field changes through a coil.
Faraday's Law: EMF = -N × dΦ/dt Where N = number of turns, Φ = magnetic flux

Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (Generator Rule):

  • Thumb: Direction of motion
  • First finger: Magnetic field (N to S)
  • Second finger: Induced current

🔥 11. Heat & Thermodynamics

Temperature & Heat

Temperature: Measure of average kinetic energy of particles.
Heat: Energy transferred between objects due to temperature difference.

Temperature Scales:

Celsius to Kelvin: K = °C + 273 Celsius to Fahrenheit: °F = (9/5 × °C) + 32

Heat Capacity & Specific Heat

Heat Capacity: Energy needed to raise temperature of object by 1°C.
C = Q/ΔT
Specific Heat Capacity: Energy needed to raise 1 kg by 1°C.
c = Q/(mΔT) Q = mcΔT

Specific Heat of Common Substances:

  • Water: 4200 J/kg°C
  • Aluminum: 900 J/kg°C
  • Iron: 450 J/kg°C
  • Lead: 130 J/kg°C

Latent Heat

Latent Heat: Energy absorbed/released during phase change without temperature change.
Q = mL Where L = latent heat of fusion or vaporization

Latent Heat Values (for water):

  • Fusion (solid→liquid): 334,000 J/kg
  • Vaporization (liquid→gas): 2,260,000 J/kg

Thermal Expansion

Linear Expansion:
ΔL = αL₀ΔT New length: L = L₀(1 + αΔT)
Volume Expansion:
ΔV = γV₀ΔT New volume: V = V₀(1 + γΔT)

Thermodynamics Laws

Zeroth Law:

If A and B are in thermal equilibrium, and B and C are in thermal equilibrium, then A and C are in thermal equilibrium.

First Law:

ΔU = Q - W Change in internal energy = Heat added - Work done by gas

Second Law:

Entropy of an isolated system increases or remains constant (never decreases). Heat flows naturally from hot to cold.

⚛️ 12. Atomic Physics & Radioactivity

Atomic Structure

Atom: Smallest unit of matter with nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in orbits.

Subatomic Particles:

Particle Location Charge Mass (u)
Proton Nucleus +1 1
Neutron Nucleus 0 1
Electron Orbit -1 1/1836

Nuclear Notation & Isotopes

Nuclear Notation:

ᴬₓX Where A = mass number (protons + neutrons) Z = atomic number (number of protons) X = element symbol
Isotopes: Same element (same Z) but different mass numbers (different neutrons). Examples: ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C (all carbon)

Radioactivity

Radioactivity: Spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei to reach stability.

Types of Radioactive Decay:

  • Alpha (α) Decay: Emission of ⁴₂He nucleus (2p + 2n) Result: Mass number ↓ 4, Atomic number ↓ 2
  • Beta (β) Decay: Emission of electron from neutron → proton Result: Mass number unchanged, Atomic number ↑ 1
  • Gamma (γ) Decay: Emission of high-energy photon Result: No change in mass or atomic number
Half-Life (t₁/₂): Time for half of radioactive sample to decay.
N = N₀ × (1/2)ⁿ Where n = number of half-lives

🔬 13. Physics Laboratory Experiments

Experiment 1: Measurement of Density Using Displacement Method
Aim:

To measure the density of an irregular solid using water displacement method.

Apparatus:
  • Measuring cylinder (100 mL)
  • Water
  • Irregular solid (stone/metal piece)
  • Balance/scale
Procedure:
  1. Measure mass of solid using balance
  2. Fill measuring cylinder with water to known level (e.g., 50 mL)
  3. Submerge solid in water gently
  4. Record new water level
  5. Volume of solid = Final volume - Initial volume
  6. Calculate density using ρ = m/V
Precautions:
  • Ensure solid is completely submerged
  • Don't pour water on solid (use string to lower it)
  • Read meniscus at eye level
  • Use accurate measuring instruments
Experiment 2: Verification of Ohm's Law
Aim:

To verify that current through a resistor is directly proportional to voltage (I ∝ V at constant R).

Apparatus:
  • Battery/Power supply (variable)
  • Resistor
  • Ammeter
  • Voltmeter
  • Rheostat (variable resistor)
  • Connecting wires
  • Switch
Circuit Diagram:

Battery → Switch → Rheostat → Resistor → Ammeter → back to Battery

Voltmeter connected in parallel across resistor

Procedure:
  1. Set up circuit as shown
  2. Close switch and adjust rheostat to different positions
  3. For each position, record voltage (V) and current (I)
  4. Take at least 5 different readings
  5. Calculate R = V/I for each reading
  6. Plot graph of I vs V (should be straight line through origin)
  7. Calculate slope = 1/R
Precautions:
  • Check ammeter polarity before use
  • Don't exceed maximum current rating
  • Close switch only when adjustments complete
  • Use appropriate ranges for meters
Experiment 3: Determination of Focal Length of Convex Lens
Aim:

To determine focal length of convex lens using lens formula.

Apparatus:
  • Convex lens
  • Optical bench with scale
  • Object (candle/object pin)
  • Screen
  • Lens holder
Procedure:
  1. Place object at one end of optical bench
  2. Place lens and screen to form real image on screen
  3. Measure object distance (u) and image distance (v)
  4. Repeat for at least 5 different object positions
  5. Use lens formula: 1/f = 1/u + 1/v to calculate f
  6. Average the focal length values
Experiment 4: Study of Simple Pendulum & Verification of T ∝ √L
Aim:

To verify that period of simple pendulum is proportional to √L (T ∝ √L).

Apparatus:
  • Simple pendulum set-up
  • Measuring scale
  • Stopwatch
  • Bob
  • Stand and clamp
Procedure:
  1. Adjust pendulum length to 20 cm
  2. Displace bob slightly and release
  3. Count 20 complete oscillations using stopwatch
  4. Calculate period T = Time/20
  5. Repeat for lengths: 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80 cm
  6. Plot graph of T vs √L (should be straight line)
Experiment 5: Laws of Reflection Using Plane Mirror
Aim:

To verify laws of reflection using plane mirror and pins.

Apparatus:
  • Plane mirror
  • Four pins of same height
  • White paper
  • Protractor
  • Ruler
Procedure:
  1. Place mirror on paper and draw line around it
  2. Draw normal perpendicular to mirror surface
  3. Place two pins in front of mirror (incident rays)
  4. Look in mirror and place two pins such that they align with images
  5. Mark positions of all pins
  6. Draw incident and reflected rays
  7. Measure angles of incidence and reflection
  8. Verify that angle of incidence = angle of reflection
  9. Repeat for different angles (at least 3 times)
Experiment 6: Determination of Specific Heat Capacity of Water
Aim:

To determine specific heat capacity of water using electrical heating.

Apparatus:
  • Calorimeter with stirrer
  • Thermometer
  • Immersion heater (electric)
  • Power supply
  • Ammeter
  • Voltmeter
  • Stopwatch
  • Balance
Procedure:
  1. Measure mass of water (m) using balance
  2. Record initial temperature (T₁)
  3. Connect heater and note voltage (V) and current (I)
  4. Switch on heater and record time (t)
  5. Stir water continuously to ensure even heating
  6. Switch off after temperature rises by 10-15°C
  7. Record final temperature (T₂)
  8. Calculate: Q = VIt (heat supplied) ΔT = T₂ - T₁ c = Q/(mΔT)
Precautions:
  • Insulate calorimeter to minimize heat loss
  • Stir water to distribute heat evenly
  • Keep heater fully immersed
  • Don't let thermometer touch bottom of container
